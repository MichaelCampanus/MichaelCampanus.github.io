Teaching Sentences, Part I

8/27/2023

Here, I mentioned teaching sentences before teaching grammar, in the pursuit of meaning. How do you actually do that?

It's not hard. Let's say that this sentence means "the girl had a lamb."

in tas lanin muuni

Obviously, you don't know which word is which, because I made those words up just now. How about "the girl had a dog?"

in sher lanin muuni

Now "the boy had a dog."

in sher gar muuni

And, of course, "the boy had a lamb."

in tas gar muuni

What about "the lamb had a girl?"

in lanin tas muuni

"The dog had a boy?"

in gar sher muuni

Picking it up? If not, let the words sink in a little. Read it a few times. Say it aloud a bit. Chew on it. In a classroom, I'd have these boys and girls and their animals drawn on the board, so I could point to them as I gave sentences, but a little deduction will have to be enough over text.

Making sense? Good. You've learned a sentence. The sentence is "a [subject] had an [object]."

Now we can talk grammar. That word "in?" It goes before the direct object. The word after "in" is the object of the sentence.

The vocabulary of the lesson:

tas = lamb
sher = dog
lanin = girl
gar = boy
muuni = had
in = direct object marker

Okay, let's put the conlang away. What did I do in that lesson?

First, a sentence. I told you what it means. You had a message, but didn't understand why it meant what it did, or any of the parts.

Then, we played with the sentence. You learned other, similar sentences, which developed into a narrowing, sharper understanding of the individual words. You had a sentence, and a little vocabulary.

Then, you learned some grammar. Now, you understand not only the sentence, but also the parts.

The sentence is basic. Meaning is basic. We want to build understanding of grammar and vocabulary on the back of sentences and meaning.
